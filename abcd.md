If your objective is to make the paper read more like the product of genuine scholarly work, focus on improving the quality of the writing itself rather than trying to bypass AI detectors. Based on the report and the excerpted paper, here are ten writing insights that will make the manuscript more authentic, analytical, and publication-ready.

1. Break the repetitive paragraph pattern.



Nearly every subsection follows the same template:

summarize Paper A,

summarize Paper B,

summarize Paper C,

conclude with "The proposed Digital Supervision Layer..."


Instead, vary the organization. Sometimes compare two papers first, sometimes discuss the evolution of the field, and sometimes begin with the research gap before discussing individual studies.

2. Reduce the frequency of announcing your own work.



Many sections conclude by immediately explaining how the proposed architecture addresses every limitation. In a survey paper, the emphasis should remain on the literature. Reserve detailed discussion of your proposed framework for the dedicated proposal section.

3. Add genuine critical analysis.



The review often lists strengths and weaknesses but rarely questions underlying assumptions. Add observations such as whether an evaluation used realistic datasets, whether scalability claims were experimentally validated, or whether deployment costs were ignored. This shows independent reasoning rather than compilation.

4. Introduce controlled uncertainty.



The manuscript frequently makes definitive statements. Researchers usually write more cautiously:

"The evidence suggests..."

"The reported results indicate..."

"It remains unclear whether..."

"This has not yet been validated at national scale."


Measured language reflects scientific thinking.

5. Vary sentence rhythm.



Many paragraphs consist of long, highly structured sentences. Mix short technical observations with longer explanations. Natural variation improves readability and avoids a mechanical style.

6. Replace repetitive transition phrases.



Expressions such as "Collectively...", "Taken together...", "Building upon these contributions...", and "The proposed Digital Supervision Layer..." appear repeatedly. Use more diverse transitions or omit them when unnecessary. Often the comparison can begin directly.

7. Include more engineering trade-off discussions.



Instead of only identifying limitations, explain why they exist. For example, discuss the trade-off between blockchain immutability and transaction cost, between public and permissioned networks, or between IoT accuracy and deployment complexity. Engineering reasoning is a hallmark of advanced research writing.

8. Show the evolution of the field.



Rather than treating each paper independently, explain how ideas progressed over time. For example, describe how early blockchain studies focused on immutability, later work adopted hybrid architectures, and recent research integrates AI and IoT. This creates a coherent narrative instead of isolated summaries.

9. Vary the depth of discussion.



Not every cited paper deserves equal attention. Spend more space on foundational or highly relevant studies and summarize less relevant papers more briefly. This demonstrates scholarly judgment and prioritization.

10. Let your research perspective emerge naturally.



Instead of repeatedly stating that your framework fills every gap, occasionally acknowledge open questions or unresolved challenges. A mature literature review recognizes that even the proposed direction has limitations and areas requiring future validation.

These changes improve the paper because they make it more analytical, nuanced, and reflective of genuine academic reasoning. They also align better with the style expected in graduate research and journal-quality survey papers, independent of any AI detection system.
