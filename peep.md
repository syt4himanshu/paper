A Literature Survey on Hybrid Blockchain-IoT Architectures for Transparent and Accountable Public Distribution Systems
Jueli Chintawar, Vishwesh Patil, Vansh Barde, Himanshu Mire: Students, Computer Engineering Department, St. Vincent Pallotti College of Engineering & Technology (SVPCET), Nagpur, India. Dr. Kapil Gupta: Associate Professor, Computer Engineering Department, St. Vincent Pallotti College of Engineering & Technology (SVPCET), Nagpur, India.
Abstract— India's Public Distribution System (PDS) delivers subsidized food grain to more than 800 million beneficiaries through nearly 500,000 Fair Price Shops, yet two decades of digitization efforts—including Aadhaar seeding, electronic point-of-sale devices, and the One Nation One Ration Card initiative—have not eliminated leakage, diversion, authentication failure, stock manipulation, or the absence of real-time oversight. This persistence points to a structural weakness rather than a lack of effort: centralized relational databases record transactions but remain mutable by design, so digitized records have not become tamper-evident or independently verifiable. This survey critically reviews and compares forty-eight sources across fifteen technology areas relevant to trustworthy welfare distribution, including blockchain-based ledgers, food-supply-chain traceability, Internet of Things-based physical verification, digital governance frameworks, biometric and token-based authentication, cryptographic hash chaining, smart contracts, hybrid blockchain-cloud architectures, artificial-intelligence-based fraud and anomaly detection, digital wallet systems, role-based access control, and cloud-hosted government computing. Rather than identifying an absence of suitable technology, the analysis reveals an integration gap: each mechanism required for a trustworthy distribution system already exists in validated form somewhere in the literature, yet no surveyed system combines more than two or three of these mechanisms into a single deployable architecture, even among the closest hybrid blockchain-cloud precedents. This finding motivates the proposed research direction—a Digital Supervision Layer that unifies hybrid blockchain anchoring, physical quantity verification through Internet of Things sensors, layered multi-factor authentication, ledger-aware role-based access control, an auditable digital wallet, and streaming rather than retrospective anomaly detection into one coherent transaction pipeline, addressing the accountability weaknesses that isolated point-solutions have consistently failed to resolve.
Keywords— blockchain, Internet of Things, public distribution system, hash-linked ledger, anomaly detection, role-based access control, multi-factor authentication, digital wallet
I. INTRODUCTION
A. Background of India's Public Distribution System
India's Public Distribution System (PDS) is among the largest food-security programmes in the world, distributing subsidised rice, wheat, sugar, and kerosene to more than 800 million beneficiaries through a network of roughly half a million Fair Price Shops (FPS) operating under state civil supply departments and the Food Corporation of India (FCI) [1]. Historically, the system relied on paper ration cards, manual stock registers, and physical verification at the shop level — a design that made the supply chain opaque at precisely the points where diversion is easiest: warehouse dispatch, transport, and shop-level dispensation [14]. The enactment of the National Food Security Act (NFSA), 2013, converted PDS entitlements into a legal right for nearly two-thirds of the population, which sharply increased the administrative and audit burden on a system that was never built for that scale of accountability [1], [3].
B. Need for Digital Transformation
The digitisation of PDS — beginning with the End-to-End Computerisation of TPDS Operations scheme and continuing through Aadhaar seeding, ePoS devices at FPS counters, and the One Nation One Ration Card (ONORC) portability initiative — was driven by the recognition that manual record-keeping could not be reconciled against real allocations in near real time [16], [13]. While ePoS rollout improved point-of-sale authentication, it did not, by itself, create a verifiable and tamper-evident record of the entire supply chain from FCI godown to beneficiary household; ePoS logs remain siloed within state-run servers that are themselves editable by the departments that operate them [14]. This gap between "digitised" and "trustworthy" is the central motivation for research into cryptographically verifiable and IoT-instrumented PDS architectures, which forms the core subject of this survey.
C. Current Challenges
Despite two decades of computerisation efforts, several structural weaknesses persist across the PDS pipeline:
Leakage — the diversion of allocated foodgrain between the warehouse and the ration shop before it reaches beneficiaries, estimated in multiple state-level studies to still affect a meaningful share of allocated stock despite ePoS adoption [3].
Diversion — grain recorded as dispensed to genuine cardholders but actually siphoned to the open market, a fraud mode that survives digitisation when the digital record itself can be falsified at the point of entry [5].
Authentication failures — biometric mismatch rates among elderly and manual-labour beneficiaries continue to cause wrongful denial of entitlements under Aadhaar-based verification [18].
Stock manipulation — mismatches between physical stock and recorded stock at FPS level, enabled by the fact that current systems record transactions but do not independently verify dispensed quantity [12].
Poor transparency — beneficiaries and civil-society auditors have no independent means of verifying that the ledger they are shown has not been altered after the fact [21].
Lack of real-time monitoring — state dashboards largely operate on periodic batch uploads rather than continuous transaction-level visibility, delaying fraud detection until well after diversion has occurred [15].
D. Motivation
Each of the challenges above traces back to a common architectural weakness: PDS digitisation to date has focused on recording transactions rather than making those records tamper-evident and independently verifiable. A centralised relational database, however well administered, is fundamentally mutable by design — any actor with sufficient database privileges can retroactively alter dispensation records without leaving a structurally enforced trace. This is precisely the property that blockchain-based ledgers, cryptographic hash chaining, and IoT-based physical verification are designed to eliminate. The motivation for the present research is therefore not to replace the existing PERN/PostgreSQL-based operational stack, but to layer a hybrid, hash-linked, blockchain-anchored audit trail atop it — one that preserves the performance and cost characteristics of a conventional relational backend while adding cryptographic non-repudiation at the points in the pipeline where fraud has historically concentrated.
E. Scope of Survey
This survey examines fifteen technology areas relevant to the design of a trustworthy digital supervision layer for PDS: blockchain applications in public distribution and food supply chains; IoT-based smart ration hardware; digital governance frameworks for welfare delivery; Aadhaar and QR-based authentication; cryptographic hash chains; Ethereum smart contracts; hybrid blockchain architectures; AI/ML-based fraud and anomaly detection; digital wallet systems; supply chain transparency mechanisms; role-based access control; and cloud-based government computing. Priority is given to work published between 2020 and 2025, supplemented by foundational papers where they remain the primary reference point for a technique. The survey deliberately excludes papers concerned purely with agricultural procurement or crop-yield forecasting, as these lie outside the distribution-and-audit scope of the proposed Digital Supervision Layer.
II. LITERATURE SURVEY
This section presents a structured and critical review of fifteen research directions that collectively inform the design of a digital supervision layer for the Public Distribution System, spanning blockchain-based trust mechanisms, Internet-of-Things (IoT) enabled physical verification, authentication and access-control schemes, and artificial-intelligence-driven anomaly detection.
A. Blockchain in Public Distribution Systems
Efforts to graft blockchain and IoT technologies onto India's ration-distribution machinery have converged on a shared diagnosis - that centralized record-keeping enables short weighing, stock diversion, and unverifiable audit trails - while diverging sharply in scale, technical depth, and empirical rigor. Malathi et al. [4] offer the most operationally mature response, deploying a private Ethereum network across Raspberry Pi nodes with Proof-of-Authority consensus rather than Proof-of-Work, and coupling it to QR-code-based ration-card authentication at IoT-enabled checkpoints so that grain movement can be tracked from warehouse to shop counter. Distinctively, the system layers a Tamil voice/text interface atop a NodeJS/REST/Web3 backend, explicitly targeting usability for illiterate and semi-literate rural beneficiaries rather than treating accessibility as an afterthought. A ten-day field trial with 80 participants in Anjur village yielded encouraging figures - full transaction immutability, roughly 130 ms response latency, 100-250 ms block-generation delay, and about 90% user satisfaction - demonstrating that blockchain-IoT integration is technically feasible even on constrained hardware [4]. Yet this feasibility was established only at village scale: throughput degrades once load exceeds roughly 40 requests per second because of fixed block size, and per-transaction delay grows with each additional node, leaving nationwide or multi-state performance unverified [4].
By contrast, Shwetha and Prabodh [5] and Singh et al. [6] operate at the conceptual rather than implementation level, and in doing so expose complementary gaps. Shwetha and Prabodh [5] propose an intelligent tracking scheme in which IoT sensors log commodity arrival and dispatch at warehouses and fair-price shops directly onto a blockchain ledger, positioning immutability and decentralization as a substitute for the existing centralized tracking apparatus; however, as a short conference contribution, it reports no throughput, latency, or deployment-cost figures, and does not address consensus design or beneficiary-side authentication [5]. Singh et al. [6] instead tackle the systemic fragmentation among government agencies, warehouses, shop owners, and beneficiaries by proposing a consortium (permissioned) blockchain with smart contracts and an explicit on-chain/off-chain split - recording trust-critical transactions on-chain while offloading bulk data off-chain to balance transparency against scalability [6]. This on-chain/off-chain philosophy anticipates the architectural pattern later required at national scale, but Singh et al. [6], like Shwetha and Prabodh [5], stop short of any prototype, field pilot, or quantitative evaluation, and neither incorporates real-time IoT-based physical verification of dispensed quantities or AI-driven anomaly detection.
Read together, these three works trace an arc from small-scale empirical validation [4] to conceptual, unvalidated architectural proposals [5], [6], collectively establishing that blockchain-IoT audit trails are desirable but leaving open how such trails scale nationally, integrate with AI-based fraud detection, or interoperate with existing digitization initiatives. The proposed Digital Supervision Layer borrows the on-chain/off-chain separation of trust-critical data from bulk records [6] and the IoT-based physical-checkpoint philosophy of [4] and [5], but implements it as a hybrid architecture anchoring only transaction hashes on Ethereum Sepolia while keeping beneficiary data in PostgreSQL, replaces ad hoc QR-checkpoint tracking with weight-verified dispensing via load-cell/HX711 hardware, and adds an AI/analytics anomaly-detection module that none of [4], [5], or [6] provide - directly addressing the scalability, AI-augmentation, and empirical-validation gaps each identifies.
B. Blockchain for Food Supply Chains
Early efforts to bring tamper-evident traceability to agri-food supply chains converged on pairing blockchain's immutable ledger with automatic data-capture technologies, and Tian's conceptual architecture for China's agri-food sector exemplifies this first wave [7]. By weighing the respective strengths and weaknesses of RFID against blockchain and then fusing them into a single layered design, the proposal enables identification, inquiry, tracking, monitoring, and tracing of goods as they move through production, processing, warehousing, distribution, and selling [7]. Its contribution lies less in empirical validation than in framing the problem: it establishes that whole-chain, multi-stakeholder trust requires both a reliable sensing mechanism at each node and a shared, tamper-resistant record of what was sensed [7]. However, being a 2016 design-stage proposal, it offers no working implementation, no throughput or latency evaluation, and no treatment of RFID cost, tag failure, or blockchain scalability, leaving the architecture unvalidated at production scale and silent on last-mile welfare distribution to individual beneficiaries [7].
Pincheira Caro et al. move decisively from concept to practice with AgriBlockIoT, a decentralized traceability system that integrates IoT data producers and consumers directly into smart contracts, deployed and benchmarked on both Ethereum and Hyperledger Sawtooth for a farm-to-fork use case [8]. Where Tian's work stops at architecture, this study quantifies real trade-offs — latency, CPU usage, and network usage — across a public versus a permissioned blockchain, providing rare comparative evidence relevant to platform selection [7], [8]. This empirical rigor comes at the cost of scope: the evaluation remains confined to a small emulated testbed rather than a multi-stakeholder national deployment, and the generic commercial use case incorporates neither AI-based anomaly detection nor beneficiary/entitlement identity verification, leaving open how such decentralized IoT-blockchain integration would generalize to subsidized public distribution [8].
Prashar et al. narrow the geographic and policy focus to India directly, proposing a smart-contract-driven traceability and visibility system for agricultural products that stores verified transactions on IPFS rather than relying on a central intermediary [9]. Their experimental evaluation, reporting 161 transactions per second with 4.82-second convergence, gives the strongest quantitative performance evidence among the three works and is the most contextually proximate to an Indian deployment [9]. Yet, like [7] and [8], it addresses general market traceability rather than ration-card entitlement verification, omits IoT-based physical quantity sensing, and leaves governance and permissioning models unspecified [9].
Collectively, [7]-[9] establish that blockchain-based traceability is both conceptually sound and practically implementable, but none extends into last-mile welfare distribution, couples blockchain with physical dispensation verification, or incorporates AI-driven anomaly detection. The proposed Digital Supervision Layer borrows the hash-anchored, tamper-evident ledger principle common to all three [7]-[9], adopts the hybrid off-chain/on-chain separation and empirical performance sensibility demonstrated in [8] and [9], and directly closes the identified gap by anchoring PDS transaction hashes on Ethereum Sepolia while adding IoT-based weight verification and AI-based anomaly detection absent from all three precedents.
C. IoT-based Smart Ration Distribution
A substantial body of embedded-systems research has sought to digitize the fair-price-shop counter itself, moving verification and dispensing away from paper ledgers and manual scooping toward sensor- and identity-driven automation. Shukla et al. [10] represent an early instance of this trend, proposing an RFID-based smart ration card in which each beneficiary's paper card is replaced by a unique RFID tag that a microcontroller-driven reader at the shop authenticates against a backend database before releasing the family's monthly quota and updating consumption records in real time. The three-tier design — tag, reader/microcontroller, and cloud database — succeeds in eliminating duplicate and ghost cards and produces a remotely auditable digital log, but the work remains a lab-scale hardware prototype: it neither engages with rural connectivity failures nor with the risk of RFID tag cloning, and, critically, it offers no mechanism for verifying that the dispensed quantity actually matches the logged entitlement, nor any tamper-evident ledger beyond a conventional database [10]. Gaikwad and Nikumbh [11] pursue a parallel but technologically distinct path, pairing a smart card encoded with household entitlement data with a GSM module that authenticates the cardholder at the shop counter and pushes SMS confirmations to the beneficiary's registered number. While this GSM-based notification loop meaningfully increases beneficiary awareness and reduces disputes over quantities received, the system inherits the fragility of cellular coverage in remote areas and, like [10], stores no biometric binding, leaving the card vulnerable to loss, duplication, or intermediary misuse, and it offers no cross-verifiable record between government, dealer, and citizen [11]. Indirani et al. [12] advance this line further by discarding the physical card altogether in favor of Aadhaar-linked fingerprint authentication combined with an STM32-controlled automated dispensing mechanism and OTP verification via GSM, logging transactions to a cloud-hosted Google Spreadsheet. This biometric binding directly addresses the impersonation and card-duplication weaknesses of [10] and [11], and sensor-controlled dispensing improves weighing precision over manual scooping; yet the choice of a spreadsheet as the system of record is itself a regression from an integrity standpoint, offering no access control granularity or tamper-evidence suitable for state-scale deployment, and the paper, like its predecessors, stops short of any distributed-ledger or AI-based anomaly detection layer [12].
Read together, these three works trace a clear trajectory — from RFID identity replacement [10], to GSM-mediated notification [11], to biometric authentication with automated dispensing [12] — each incrementally hardening beneficiary identification and shop-counter automation, yet all sharing the same structural blind spot: none verifies dispensed weight against entitlement in a way that is independently auditable, and none anchors transaction records in a tamper-evident, multi-stakeholder ledger. The proposed Digital Supervision Layer borrows the core insight of embedded, sensor-driven verification at the point of dispensing from [10]-[12], but replaces card- or biometric-only authentication with an ESP32/load-cell/HX711 weighing subsystem that gates transaction confirmation on measured-versus-entitled weight tolerance, while avoiding the fragile centralized data stores of [10]-[12] by anchoring transaction hashes on the Ethereum Sepolia blockchain and layering AI-based anomaly detection absent from all three prior systems.
